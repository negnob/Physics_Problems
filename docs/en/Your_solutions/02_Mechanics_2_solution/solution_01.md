## 1. Gravitational Dependence

A simple pendulum has period

$$T=4\text{ s}$$

on Earth.

We need:

- the period on the Moon, where $g_M=\frac{g_E}{6}$
- the length of a pendulum with period exactly $1\text{ s}$ on Earth

---

## Useful Definitions and Formulas

For a simple pendulum,

$$T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

So:
- the period is proportional to $\frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}$
- if the length is fixed, then

$$T\propto\frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}$$

Rearranging for length:

$$L=g\left(\frac{T}{2\pi}\right)^2$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### Part A. Period on the Moon

Since the length stays the same,

$$\frac{T_M}{T_E}=\sqrt{\frac{g_E}{g_M}}$$

But

$$g_M=\frac{g_E}{6}$$

So

$$\frac{T_M}{T_E}=\sqrt{\frac{g_E}{g_E/6}}=\sqrt{6}$$

Thus

$$T_M=T_E\sqrt{6}=4\sqrt{6}$$

Numerically,

$$T_M\approx4\cdot2.449\approx9.80\text{ s}$$

---

### Part B. Length for period $1\text{ s}$ on Earth

Use

$$L=g\left(\frac{T}{2\pi}\right)^2$$

Take

$$T=1\text{ s},\qquad g=9.8\text{ m/s}^2$$

Then

$$L=9.8\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\right)^2$$

$$L=\frac{9.8}{4\pi^2}$$

Numerically,

$$L\approx\frac{9.8}{39.48}\approx0.248\text{ m}$$

---

## Final Answer

Period on the Moon:

$$\boxed{T_M=4\sqrt{6}\text{ s}\approx9.80\text{ s}}$$

Required length for $T=1\text{ s}$ on Earth:

$$\boxed{L\approx0.248\text{ m}}$$
