## 1. Wave Properties

### Useful definitions and formulas

For a wave, the basic relation is:

$$v=f\lambda$$

This means:

- $v$ is the wave speed
- $f$ is the frequency
- $\lambda$ is the wavelength

This formula shows the connection between three important wave quantities:

- **wave speed** tells us how fast the wave travels through the medium
- **frequency** tells us how many wave cycles pass in one second
- **wavelength** tells us the length of one full wave

If we want to find wavelength, we rearrange the formula:

$$\lambda=\frac{v}{f}$$

This means:

- we divide the speed by the frequency
- because wavelength is the distance covered during one oscillation
- if the frequency stays the same and the speed becomes larger, then each wave must become longer, so the wavelength increases

Important idea:

When a sound wave goes from air into water:

- the **frequency does not change**
- the **speed changes**
- so the **wavelength changes**

This happens because the frequency is determined by the source of the sound, not by the medium.  
If the source produces $440$ vibrations every second, then the wave will keep this same frequency in both air and water.  
But the medium affects how fast the wave travels, so the wavelength changes.

---

### Step-by-step solution

We are given:

- $f=440\text{ Hz}$
- speed in air: $v_{\text{air}}=343\text{ m/s}$
- speed in water: $v_{\text{water}}=1482\text{ m/s}$

We need to find:

- the wavelength in air
- the wavelength in water

Since wavelength is not given directly, we use:

$$\lambda=\frac{v}{f}$$

---

### 1) Find the wavelength in air

Use the formula:

$$\lambda=\frac{v}{f}$$

For air:

$$\lambda_{\text{air}}=\frac{343}{440}$$

Explanation of this step:

- we substitute the speed of sound in air instead of $v$
- we substitute the frequency $440\text{ Hz}$ instead of $f$
- then we divide to find the wavelength

Now divide:

$$\lambda_{\text{air}}=0.7795\text{ m}$$

Rounded:

$$\lambda_{\text{air}}\approx0.78\text{ m}$$

More explanation:

- this means one full sound wave in air has a length of about $0.78$ meters
- in other words, from one compression of the sound wave to the next compression, the distance is about $0.78$ m
- the wavelength is not very large in air because the speed of sound in air is not extremely high

---

### 2) Find the wavelength in water

Use the same formula:

$$\lambda=\frac{v}{f}$$

For water:

$$\lambda_{\text{water}}=\frac{1482}{440}$$

Explanation of this step:

- now we use the speed of sound in water
- the frequency stays the same, because the source still produces the same sound
- only the speed changes, so the wavelength must also change

Now divide:

$$\lambda_{\text{water}}=3.368\text{ m}$$

Rounded:

$$\lambda_{\text{water}}\approx3.37\text{ m}$$

More explanation:

- this means one full sound wave in water has a length of about $3.37$ meters
- this wavelength is much larger than in air
- the reason is that sound travels much faster in water, so during one oscillation the wave covers a much greater distance

---

### 3) Compare the two answers

We found:

$$\lambda_{\text{air}}\approx0.78\text{ m}$$

$$\lambda_{\text{water}}\approx3.37\text{ m}$$

Now we compare them:

- the wavelength in water is much bigger than the wavelength in air
- this is because the speed in water is much greater
- the frequency stayed the same in both cases

So the physical idea is:

- same source $\to$ same frequency
- different medium $\to$ different speed
- different speed $\to$ different wavelength

---

### Final answer

$$\lambda_{\text{air}}\approx0.78\text{ m}$$

$$\lambda_{\text{water}}\approx3.37\text{ m}$$
